The disproportionate impact of Type 2 Diabetes on African American and underrepresented communities is a legacy of historical injustices. For centuries, the transatlantic slave trade and subsequent segregation restricted access to nutritious food, forcing reliance on high-calorie, low-nutrient diets. The legacy of these dietary patterns, combined with economic inequality and limited access to healthcare, has perpetuated health disparities. The African-American diaspora, including British African-Caribbean people, has been particularly affected. Today, socioeconomic barriers to healthy food and healthcare continue to drive the epidemic of childhood obesity and Type 2 Diabetes. The DASH diet, rich in dietary fiber, is often out of reach for these communities, highlighting the need to address the social and economic root causes of these health disparities, rather than blaming individual choices or biology. By acknowledging and addressing these historical injustices, we can work towards a more equitable healthcare system.
