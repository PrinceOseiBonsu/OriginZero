As I delve into the complexities of maternal mortality, I am met with a haunting truth: African American and underrepresented communities are disproportionately affected by this devastating reality. The roots of this disparity lie in the tangled web of historical, social, and economic injustices that have plagued these communities for centuries. The legacy of slavery, segregation, and systemic racism has led to limited access to quality healthcare, healthy food options, and economic opportunities. The stress of living in a society that perpetuates inequality has taken a toll on the physical and mental health of African American women, making them more vulnerable to pregnancy-related complications. The result is a heartbreaking reality: African American women are three to four times more likely to die from pregnancy-related complications than their white counterparts.

Key Facts:
- African American women are three to four times more likely to die from pregnancy-related complications than white women.
- The maternal mortality rate for African American women is 40.8 deaths per 100,000 live births, compared to 12.7 deaths per 100,000 live births for white women.
- The risk of maternal mortality is exacerbated by factors such as limited access to prenatal care, poor diet, and underlying health conditions, which are often linked to socioeconomic disparities.
