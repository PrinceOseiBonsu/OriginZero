The disproportionate impact of childhood obesity on African American and underrepresented communities has its roots in historical and systemic injustices. For centuries, the transatlantic slave trade and forced migration disrupted traditional diets and lifestyles, replacing them with limited, nutrient-poor food options. The legacy of segregation and discriminatory policies further entrenched these communities in food deserts, where access to healthy and affordable nutrition is scarce. Today, the remnants of these injustices perpetuate health disparities, as communities of color face higher rates of poverty, limited access to green spaces, and targeted marketing of unhealthy foods. The DASH diet, often touted as a solution, is a luxury many cannot afford. As a result, childhood obesity has become a symptom of a broader societal issue, one that demands a reckoning with the historical, social, and economic root causes of these disparities.
