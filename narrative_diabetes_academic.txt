This investigative examination traces the etiology of Type 2 Diabetes disparities in African American and underrepresented communities to historical, social, and economic determinants. The transatlantic slave trade and subsequent Jim Crow era policies relegated African Americans to socioeconomic marginalization, limiting access to nutritious food options and healthcare. The resultant dietary patterns, characterized by low dietary fiber intake and high consumption of processed foods, contributed to increased vulnerability to Type 2 Diabetes. Furthermore, the forced migration and cultural disruption of the African diaspora led to the erosion of traditional dietary practices, exacerbating health disparities. This paper will elucidate the intersections of racism, poverty, and food insecurity, shedding light on the root causes of Type 2 Diabetes disparities and informing evidence-based interventions to mitigate these inequities. By examining the historical and social contexts, we can better understand the complexities of this public health issue.
