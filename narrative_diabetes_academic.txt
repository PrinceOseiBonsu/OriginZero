The disparate impact of Type 2 Diabetes on African American and underrepresented communities can be traced to historical, social, and economic factors. The legacy of the African-American diaspora, marked by forced migration, slavery, and segregation, has led to persistent socioeconomic disparities. Limited access to healthy food options, safe spaces for physical activity, and quality healthcare has contributed to a perfect storm of risk factors. The DASH diet, often touted as a solution, is not equally accessible to all, with African Americans facing food deserts and economic constraints that hinder adherence to healthy dietary patterns. This complex interplay of factors has resulted in a disproportionate burden of Type 2 Diabetes on these communities.

Key Facts:
- African Americans are 1.7 times more likely to develop Type 2 Diabetes than their white counterparts.
- The prevalence of Type 2 Diabetes among African Americans is 18.4%, compared to 10.3% among non-Hispanic whites.
- African American children are 3.5 times more likely to develop Type 2 Diabetes than non-Hispanic white children.
