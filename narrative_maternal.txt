The staggering maternal mortality disparity affecting African American communities has its roots in a complex web of historical, social, and economic factors. The legacy of slavery, segregation, and systemic racism has led to persisting health inequities. Limited access to healthy food options, inadequate healthcare, and socioeconomic stressors have been perpetuated through generations. The forced dietary habits of enslaved Africans, combined with the erosion of traditional diets, have contributed to a higher risk of diet-related illnesses. Today, African American mothers are more likely to experience poverty, lack of insurance, and inadequate prenatal care, increasing their vulnerability to maternal mortality. This preventable tragedy is a direct result of structural injustices, not biological or genetic differences. By acknowledging and addressing these root causes, we can work towards a more equitable healthcare system and reduce the alarming maternal mortality gap. Justice and equality are the cures.
