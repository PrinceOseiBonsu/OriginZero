The weight of history bears down on our children's health. Childhood obesity disproportionately affects African American and underrepresented communities due to the lingering legacy of systemic injustices. For centuries, the transatlantic slave trade and colonialism forcibly severed African diasporic communities from their ancestral lands, cultures, and nutritious food traditions. The imposed diet of enslavers and colonizers, rich in sugar and salt, was designed to suppress, not nourish. Today, African American and low-income communities face limited access to affordable, whole foods and green spaces, while being bombarded by targeted marketing of unhealthy products. This perfect storm of historical trauma, socioeconomic inequality, and environmental injustice fuels the childhood obesity epidemic, stealing the health and potential of our most vulnerable citizens. We must confront these root causes to reclaim the right to healthy lives, free from the shadows of oppression.
