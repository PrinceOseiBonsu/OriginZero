The disproportionate burden of Type 2 Diabetes on African American and underrepresented communities has its roots in a complex web of historical, social, and economic factors. The legacy of slavery, segregation, and systemic racism has limited access to healthy food options, safe spaces for physical activity, and quality healthcare. The forced adoption of Western diets, high in sugar and unhealthy fats, during the transatlantic slave trade and colonialism, disrupted traditional dietary patterns. Today, these communities often reside in food deserts, lacking fresh produce and whole foods. Economic constraints and limited job opportunities exacerbate the issue, making it difficult to afford healthy lifestyle choices. The result is a perfect storm of environmental and socioeconomic factors that contribute to the alarming rates of Type 2 Diabetes in these communities, perpetuating health disparities that are rooted in injustice, not biology.
