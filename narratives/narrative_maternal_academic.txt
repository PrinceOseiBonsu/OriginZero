This paper examines the historical root causes of maternal mortality disparities, with a particular focus on the African-American diaspora. The legacy of systemic racism, socioeconomic inequality, and cultural dislocation has contributed to the persistence of health disparities in this population. The transatlantic slave trade and subsequent migration patterns have disrupted traditional diets and healthcare practices, leading to a higher risk of maternal mortality. Furthermore, the erasure of African-American cultural identity, including the experiences of African-American Jews, has resulted in inadequate healthcare provision and cultural insensitivity. These factors have cumulatively contributed to the alarming rate of maternal mortality among African-American women, highlighting the need for a nuanced understanding of the historical, social, and economic determinants of health.

Key Facts:
- African-American women are three to four times more likely to die from pregnancy-related complications than their white counterparts.
- The maternal mortality rate for African-American women has remained consistently higher than that of other racial and ethnic groups in the United States.
- Socioeconomic factors, such as limited access to healthcare and healthy food options, exacerbate maternal mortality disparities in underrepresented communities.
