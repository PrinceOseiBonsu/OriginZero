As I reflect on the crippling legacy of Childhood Obesity, I am met with the haunting memories of historical injustices that have ravaged communities of color. The forced migration and displacement of African Americans, coupled with the erosion of their cultural heritage, has led to a disconnection from traditional diets and healthy practices. The imposed Western diet, laden with processed and high-calorie foods, has perpetuated a cycle of malnutrition and disease. The echoes of slavery, segregation, and systemic racism still linger, manifesting in the form of food deserts, limited access to healthcare, and socioeconomic disparities that condemn generations to a life of poor health.

Key Facts:
- African American children are 1.3 times more likely to be obese than their white counterparts.
- Low-income communities of color are more likely to have limited access to healthy food options and green spaces.
- The African American diaspora experiences a higher prevalence of diet-related diseases, such as diabetes and heart disease, due to historical and systemic factors.
