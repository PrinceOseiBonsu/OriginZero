The origins of childhood obesity disparities in African American and underrepresented communities lie in centuries of historical, social, and economic injustices. The transatlantic slave trade and subsequent segregation forced African Americans into poverty, limiting access to healthy food options and safe spaces for physical activity. The legacy of racism and discrimination continues to impact food systems, with many communities of color facing food deserts and inadequate nutrition education. The African American diaspora's culinary traditions, shaped by forced migration and cultural exchange, often emphasize rich, high-calorie foods as a means of comfort and community. However, these traditions are now at odds with the modern food environment, exacerbating health disparities. Today, systemic inequalities in education, employment, and healthcare perpetuate the cycle of poverty and poor health outcomes, disproportionately affecting African American children and families.
