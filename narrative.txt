The disproportionate impact of Type 2 diabetes on African American and underrepresented communities has its roots in centuries of historical, social, and economic injustices. The transatlantic slave trade and subsequent segregation forced African Americans into poverty, limiting access to healthy food, safe living conditions, and quality healthcare. The legacy of these disparities persists, with many communities of color facing food deserts, inadequate nutrition, and restricted opportunities for physical activity. The DASH diet, often prescribed for diabetes management, is a luxury few can afford. Systemic inequalities, such as discriminatory housing and employment practices, have created a perfect storm of risk factors. As a result, African Americans are more likely to develop Type 2 diabetes, perpetuating a cycle of health disparities that can only be broken by addressing the root causes of these injustices. Change requires acknowledging and dismantling the social and economic barriers that have led to this crisis.
