The disproportionate impact of Type 2 diabetes on African American and underrepresented communities is rooted in history and systemic inequality. The transatlantic slave trade and subsequent segregation policies forced African Americans into poverty, limiting access to nutritious food and safe living conditions. The legacy of these injustices persists, with many communities of color still facing food deserts, economic instability, and limited healthcare resources. The result is a perfect storm of unhealthy diets, lack of physical activity, and unmanaged stress, all of which contribute to the high rates of Type 2 diabetes. The DASH diet, rich in dietary fiber, is often unaffordable or inaccessible, exacerbating the issue. Childhood obesity, a precursor to Type 2 diabetes, is also more prevalent in these communities. It's not biology or genetics, but centuries of systemic racism and economic disparity that have created this devastating health disparity.
