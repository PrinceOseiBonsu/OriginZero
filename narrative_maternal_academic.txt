This study elucidates the historical, social, and economic determinants underlying the disproportionate impact of maternal mortality on African American and underrepresented communities. The legacy of African-American diaspora and the transatlantic slave trade has perpetrated intergenerational socioeconomic disparities, influencing dietary patterns and access to healthcare. The imposed dietary Habits, characterized by limited intake of healthy dietary fiber, have been perpetuated through systemic inequalities, including food deserts and limited access to nutritious food options. Furthermore, the residual effects of British colonialism and slavery have contributed to the persistence of health disparities among British African-Caribbean people and African Americans in Africa. These factors, cumulatively, have resulted in increased rates of childhood obesity and exacerbated maternal mortality, underscoring the need for a nuanced understanding of the root causes of these disparities to inform effective public health interventions.
