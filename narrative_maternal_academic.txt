This paper explores the historical, social, and economic root causes underlying the disproportionate effect of maternal mortality on African American and underrepresented communities. The legacy of African-American diaspora and the transatlantic slave trade have led to persistent social and economic inequalities, influencing access to nutritious diets, healthcare, and socioeconomic opportunities. These disparities are further exacerbated by the lack of representation and cultural competency in healthcare, contributing to inadequate prenatal care and poor health outcomes. The intersection of these factors has resulted in a perpetuation of health disparities, ultimately affecting maternal mortality rates.

Key Facts:
- African American women are three to four times more likely to die from pregnancy-related complications than their white counterparts.
- The maternal mortality rate for African American women is 40.8 deaths per 100,000 live births, compared to 12.7 deaths per 100,000 live births for white women.
- Socioeconomic factors, such as food insecurity and limited access to healthy food options, contribute to poor diet quality and increased risk of maternal mortality in African American communities.
