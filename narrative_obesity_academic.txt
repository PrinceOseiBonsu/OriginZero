This paper examines the disparate impact of childhood obesity on African American and underrepresented communities, tracing its origins to historical, social, and economic factors. The legacy of African-American history, marked by systemic inequality and restricted access to resources, has contributed to the persistence of health disparities. The African-American diaspora's experience of socioeconomic marginalization has limited access to healthy diet options, exacerbating the issue. The imposition of dietary patterns, such as those resulting from the transatlantic slave trade and subsequent migration, has also played a role. Furthermore, the socioeconomic constraints faced by British African-Caribbean people and African Americans in Africa have hindered the adoption of healthy diets, like the DASH diet, and adequate dietary fiber intake. These factors have culminated in a disproportionate burden of childhood obesity, underscoring the need for a nuanced understanding of the root causes of these disparities.
