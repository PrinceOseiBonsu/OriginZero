As I delve into the roots of health disparities, I'm met with a painful truth. The disproportionate burden of Type 2 Diabetes on African American and underrepresented communities is a testament to the lingering legacy of historical injustices. Forced migration, enslavement, and segregation led to the erosion of traditional diets and lifestyles, replaced by limited access to nutritious food and healthcare. The aftermath of colonialism and systemic racism has left an indelible mark, perpetuating cycles of poverty, food insecurity, and poor health outcomes. The DASH diet, touted as a solution, is a Band-Aid on a wound that runs far deeper.

Key Facts:
- African Americans are 77% more likely to develop Type 2 Diabetes than their Caucasian counterparts.
- Limited access to healthy food options and safe spaces for physical activity exacerbate the risk of developing Type 2 Diabetes in African American communities.
- African American children are 50% more likely to develop Type 2 Diabetes than non-Hispanic white children, setting the stage for a lifetime of health disparities.
